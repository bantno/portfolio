AHAB (Aquatic High-endurance Aerial Biomonitor) is a solar-powered seaplane UAV designed for persistent marine mammal monitoring over remote ocean regions. The project spans conceptual vehicle design with a novel wing-rotation mechanism, an MDP-based flight planning framework that outperforms heuristic approaches, and a phased prototype flight test campaign.

<section id="motivation">

## The Problem

Cetacean species — whales, dolphins, and porpoises — are experiencing sustained population declines worldwide, and marine biologists need better tools to study and protect them. Current monitoring platforms each have critical limitations: ship-based surveys are expensive and weather-dependent, fixed-wing UAVs like ScanEagle require manual launch and recovery with limited range, surface vehicles like SailDrone are too slow for focal-animal tracking, and passive acoustic buoys cannot be repositioned. A solar-powered seaplane fills this gap by combining persistent deployment through water landings and solar recharging, sufficient airspeed for focal monitoring, autonomous operation that eliminates ship-based recovery costs, and dual-modality sensing — visual survey while airborne, acoustic monitoring via hydrophone while floating.

## Conceptual Design

The seaplane's design integrates aerodynamic performance, hydrostatic stability, and solar energy harvesting into a single airframe. A flying hull configuration was selected over floatplane and amphibious alternatives for its reduced structural weight and better takeoff performance in rough seas.

The design process iterates through geometry definition, mass estimation, parasitic drag analysis in OpenVSP, endurance calculation, and hydrostatic stability checks until all requirements are met. Key geometric parameters — wing span, beam width, deadrise angle, and forebody/sternpost proportions — are tuned to balance flight efficiency against water stability.

<ExplodedAssembly client:visible layers={seaplaneLayers} title="Seaplane Assembly" basePath={basePath} />

A custom spanwise wing rotation mechanism enables solar tracking, yielding a 21.9% energy gain over fixed panels. Solar modeling with pvlib and Ascent Solar CIGS thin-film module parameters shows the vehicle can sustain 1.3 hours of flight per day in January and 2.3 hours in June at the target deployment latitude — meeting the mission requirement of at least 1 hour of flight per day year-round.

## Optimal Flight Planning

A solar-powered seaplane must decide when to fly, when to rest on the water and recharge, and how to balance observation time against the risk of battery exhaustion or crash. Weather is stochastic — solar irradiance and wind speed vary unpredictably — so simple threshold rules ("fly when battery is above X, land when below Y") are intuitive but leave performance on the table.

I formulated this as a Markov Decision Process and solved it via stochastic dynamic programming. The vehicle's state captures its battery level and operating mode (floating, flying, or failed), and at each time step the optimal policy selects from takeoff, cruise, land, or save energy based on current conditions. The value function is computed offline and stored onboard for real-time decision-making.

The interactive demo below lets you try to design a threshold rule that outperforms the optimal policy — the key insight is that the MDP makes non-obvious decisions, like resting during a sunny period to build energy reserves for a risky overnight segment, that no simple threshold can replicate.

<MDPDemo client:visible />

Monte Carlo sensitivity studies across battery capacities, failure penalties, geographic locations, and seasonal conditions show the optimal policy consistently outperforms all threshold parameter combinations. Unlike threshold controllers that require location-specific tuning, the MDP adapts automatically to different environments.

## Prototype: AHAB

AHAB is a proof-of-concept prototype built to demonstrate that the airframe can execute the types of missions the MDP framework plans. Development follows a phased flight test approach: aerodynamic validation, hydrostatic and hydrodynamic validation, subsystem integration, and full mission demonstration.

The airframe is 3D-printed in LW ASA, reinforced with carbon fiber spars and fiberglass, and waterproofed with marine-grade sealant and electronics potting. Modifications from the conceptual design include a V-tail for easier construction and sponsons for improved hydrostatic stability.

<Figure src="/images/seaplane/Preflight.jpg" alt="AHAB prototype during pre-flight inspection" caption="AHAB during pre-flight inspection" number={4} wide={true} />

### Phase 1: Aerodynamic Validation

Phase 1 confirmed the airframe is flightworthy. AHAB flew with minimal trim corrections, significant excess thrust, and excess control authority in cruise. Measured performance: 3.75 kg all-up weight, ~150W cruise power at 17 m/s and 35% throttle, glide ratio of 6.5, and a 4–5 second takeoff run.

### Phase 2: Hydrostatic & Hydrodynamic Validation

Phase 2 validates water operations. The second prototype is fully waterproofed and hydrostatically stable. Waterproofing tests and low-speed taxi runs are complete. Remaining evaluation includes hump speed, planing behavior, step ventilation, and water takeoff.
